The Four Quadrants — How This Works

Each quadrant below is defined by two variables: whether growth is coming in above or below what's priced in, and whether inflation is coming in above or below what's priced in. It's the surprise relative to expectations that moves asset prices — not the absolute level. That's why a "weak" economy that's still beating depressed expectations behaves like a growth-up quadrant, not a growth-down one.

Volatility and correlation for US Equities, International Equities, Nominal Bonds, Commodities, Gold, and Cash are computed directly from real historical annual total returns, 2001–2024 (S&P 500, MSCI EAFE, 10-yr Treasury, 3-month T-bill, gold — Damodaran/NYU Stern dataset; commodities — Bloomberg Commodity Index). International Equities uses MSCI EAFE (developed ex-US) as a proxy — a real VXUS allocation also includes emerging markets, which this slightly understates. TIPS lacks a clean long public return series here, so its figures remain an estimate, not computed from data. Both risk parity buttons exclude Cash from the optimization — its near-zero volatility would otherwise force almost all weight into cash, which is why real risk parity funds treat cash as the leverage funding leg rather than a risk-parity target. Adjust the Cash slider manually if you want a buffer.

Risk Contribution

Dollar weight ≠ risk weight. This shows each asset's actual marginal contribution to total portfolio variance at your current allocation — accounting for correlation, not just volatility — which is the thing risk parity is solving for.

Leverage (Optional)

Risk-balancing shrinks expected return because it shifts dollars from high-return equities into low-volatility bonds/cash. Bridgewater's real All Weather approach restores return by leveraging the whole risk-balanced mix up to a target volatility. This is optional here — toggle off to see your allocation unlevered.
